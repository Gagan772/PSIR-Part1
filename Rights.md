# Natural Rights and Human Rights

## Intro

**Harold J. Laski** — Rights are those conditions of life, without which no man can seek to be at his best (*Grammar of Politics*)

---

## Three Generations of Rights (Karel Vasak)

### 1️⃣ First Generation — Civil & Political Rights
- Related to liberty and political participation  
- Core rights: Life, Liberty, Property (John Locke)  
- **Negative rights** → Require restraint on others  

### 2️⃣ Second Generation — Socio-Economic Rights
- Inspired by socialism and social justice  
- Right to work, health, education  
- **Positive rights** → Require state intervention  

### 3️⃣ Third Generation — Cultural & Developmental Rights
- Linked to multiculturalists (Bhikhu Parekh)  
- Collective/people’s rights  
- Self-determination, peace, environment, multicultural rights

# Natural Rights

- Based on **liberal social contract theory**
- Rights exist **prior to the state**
- State formed to protect natural rights  
- **John Locke — Second Treatise on Civil Government**
  - Individual = end, state = means  
  - Natural rights: Life, Liberty, Property  

### Nature of Natural Rights

- Self-evident truths  
- Man born with them  
- Do not depend on state recognition  
- Based on reason, not institution  
- Theological basis (American Declaration of Independence)

### Traditions within Natural Rights

#### 1️⃣ Social Contract Tradition
- Rights existed in state of nature  
- Inalienable  
- Not transferred to state  

**Exponents**: Locke, Rousseau  

#### 2️⃣ Thomas Paine
- Book: *Rights of Man*  
- Critic of social contract binding future generations  

---

## Criticism of Natural Rights

### 1️⃣ Legal Positivists (Bentham, Hobbes)

- Source of rights = law/state  
- “Nonsense upon stilts” (Bentham)  
- Abstract, uncertain  
- Recipe for lawlessness  
- Hobbes → No rights in state of nature  
- Bentham → Utilitarian view  
- Natural rights = “anarchical fallacies”  
- Only state provides legal support  

### 2️⃣ Cultural Rights Theory (Edmund Burke)

- Source of rights = culture, not nature  
- Rights need social sanction  
- Cannot challenge customs  
- Criticizes abstract individualism

# Human Rights

- Rights people have **by virtue of being human** (Thomas Paine)  
- Universal  
- Post-WW2 concept (Nazi atrocities)

### Features (EUIIP)

- Equality  
- Universality  
- Indivisibility  
- Inalienability  
- Permanence  

- Available irrespective of caste, religion, race, sex  
- UDHR + ICCPR 1966 + ICESCR = International Bill of Rights  

### James Nickel

- Human rights = non-negotiable ingredient of human identity  
- Universal, fundamental, absolute  

- Modern secular version of natural rights  
- Broader than natural rights

## Criticism of Human Rights

- Crisis of legitimacy  
- Used for Western geopolitical interests  
- Selective enforcement  
- Clash of Civilisations (Huntington)  
- No consensus on minimum rights  
- Cultural relativism (Franz Boas)

### Cultural Relativists

- Culture shapes rights  
- No culture superior/inferior  
- Rights cannot be culture-independent
